Issue #14 — The tools are splitting

Apr 7 → 14
The big story

Cursor 3 is here — and it's built around agents, not code

Cursor shipped a completely new interface this week — and it's a big philosophical shift. Instead of one AI helping you write code, the new Agents Window lets you run multiple agents in parallel across local and cloud environments. The interesting part for designers: Design Mode lets you click directly on UI elements in the browser and tell the agent what to change, visually. Worth knowing about even if you don't code — this is where the "design in the browser" conversation is heading. The HN reaction was mixed: top comment said "I still want to code." A Cursor engineer replied: the old IDE still works. Both can coexist.

Figma opens write access to AI agents

Figma now lets AI agents create and edit real design assets in your files — not just read them. Agents can use your actual components, variables, and tokens. They also introduced Skills, which are markdown files that teach agents how your team specifically works. It's free during the beta. Bitovi ran the first structured test and found components worked well but auto-layout was inconsistent. Worth experimenting with now while it's free.

Figma Blog Bitovi test 📡 171 mentions/mo

Lovable hit $200M ARR in one year

Lovable went from zero to $200M ARR in one year, which is genuinely rare. The pitch is simple: describe what you want in plain English and get a working app. Designers and PMs are using it to skip the developer queue entirely — build a prototype, show stakeholders, iterate. The Lenny's Podcast episode on their growth story is worth a listen if you're curious about where no-code tools are actually heading.

Lenny's Podcast 💰 $200M ARR 📅 1 year

Our read: Two very different bets on the same future. Cursor is building for developers who want to manage agent fleets. Lovable is building for everyone else who just wants to skip straight to a working app. Both are growing fast — which probably means both are right, just for different people.

What the community is saying

"This design process that designers have been taught, we sort of treat it as gospel. That's basically dead."

— Jenny Wen (head of design, Claude/Anthropic), Lenny's Podcast

"Designers are not being replaced. They are being sorted."

This was the most-shared design × AI piece this month, and it's worth reading in full. It pulls from Figma's hiring data: 73% of design managers now want AI-tool proficiency, 79% want people who can design AI products, and over half are only hiring at the senior level. The framing that stuck: there are now three designer archetypes — the senior with deep product judgment, the one close to code, and the "cracked new grad" who has no attachment to old processes. Two groups are forming, and the gap between them grows every month.

"Operating as an AI-native designer in 2026"

If you read one thing about how the design role is actually changing day-to-day, make it this. A designer at an AI-native company explains his workflow: Cursor + Claude Code + a coded design system. No Figma-first process. His take: "The shift is from making to deciding — AI gives you solid starting points, and your job is evaluating what's actually worth building."

Only 31% of designers use AI for core work

Some actual numbers on AI adoption in design: only 31% of designers use AI for core work, compared to 59% of developers. That's a big gap. The report is worth skimming — the most interesting finding is that the designers most at risk aren't the ones ignoring AI, but the ones whose entire value was pixel-level execution.

Humbl Design 📊 data report 📅 2026

The pattern across all of these: the role isn't shrinking — it's splitting. One track leads toward strategy, evaluation, and system-level thinking. The other track, pure execution without judgment, is what AI is getting good at. Every source we read this week arrived at the same conclusion from a different angle.

Tools & workflows

Onlook: "Cursor for designers"

This one's worth trying if you work in React. It lets you drag, rearrange, and resize elements directly in your running app — visually, like Figma — but it's editing real code underneath. No terminal needed. It's open source and free. One designer called it "Figma and v0 fused together." If you've been curious about designing in code, this is probably the gentlest on-ramp right now.

"I tried 5 vibe-coding tools. Here's what actually ships."

Someone actually tested Lovable, Bolt, Replit, v0, and Cursor on the same project over a week — and wrote up what happened honestly. Lovable looked gorgeous in 4 minutes but broke on real logic. Replit was messy but the end result was solid. v0 is great if you already code. Cursor isn't for non-coders. The takeaway: use Lovable to prototype, then bring in a developer for production. That's still a huge improvement over two years ago.

The bottom line

The design tool landscape is fragmenting in interesting ways. Cursor built an agent control room with visual feedback for designers. Lovable proved that non-developers can ship real apps. Onlook merged Figma's editing model with actual codebases. And Figma opened its canvas to agents while navigating a credit pricing backlash.

The thread connecting all of it: the designer's value is moving from production to judgment. Deciding what's worth building. Writing the rules that agents follow. Evaluating whether the output is actually good enough. That's not a smaller role — it's a harder one, and a more interesting one.
